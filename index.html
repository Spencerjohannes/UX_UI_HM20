Problem Statement

Austin Area Arts is a non-profit organization that merges three entities including an arts center that offers classes, an annual arts festival, and a historic theater. The existing website lacks a streamlined and intuitive user flow for the local community to find events, view class information, or inquire for details about the festival. Due to the absence of understandable navigational patterns, users are not able to effectively engage with the site. How might we improve the navigation of the home page to allow users to easily locate the information they need?

Goals

1

The revamped website intends to make it easier for users to locate information on events, classes, and the yearly arts festival, with higher engagement metrics suggesting positive involvement.

2

Steps will be made to provide the target demographic with streamlined access to information about events, classes, and festival queries on the homepage, with the goal of reducing the number of clicks or steps required for efficient information retrieval.

3

The major goal is to receive positive feedback from users and stakeholders, along with higher satisfaction levels, indicating a pleasant and effective overall user experience.

My main objective during the research phase was to comprehensively grasp user needs and challenges, providing valuable insights to inform the redesign of the Austin Area Arts website. Through diligent research and analysis, we pinpointed the underlying causes of navigation issues and unearthed additional user preferences related to layout, navigation, and information accessibility.

Card Sorting

We used card sorting to improve homepage navigation by removing extraneous pages such as "Lighten the Load" and "Family Movie Night." We merged web pages such as support, store, give, and support festival into a user-friendly "Give" button/ webpage and renamed misleading web pages like "Class Shop"to "Classes" and "Art Lesson"to "Private Lesson."

Site Map

Our primary goal for this project was to improve the website's navigation patterns, therefore we decided to update the layout by designing a new site map that helps visualize the improved website structure and organization. It provides a simple and clear summary of the UX/UI design changes, showcasing the effectiveness of our decisions.

Test: Validation, Usability, Feedback

The project's extensive feedback and testing greatly enhanced design alignment with actual user needs. Usability studies and stakeholder feedback identified navigation issues, preferences for color-coded banners and multiple cards, and concerns about distracting aspects. A/B testing found that users preferred two cards and visual coherence with aligned banners. This iterative method stressed the necessity of continual refining based on user insights, resulting in an improved overall user experience.

Challenges & Conclusion

1. Chaotic Figma file

This impacted our cooperation efforts. To solve this, we built a label system and ordered page layouts, which required more effort and collaboration but dramatically improved overall workflow.

2. Teammates lacking Program skills

Managing differing skill levels among teammates necessitated several reformatting efforts. To increase productivity, I started knowledge-sharing meetings and set explicit formatting requirements. Despite the time spent on rework, I saw the value of early training and consistent processes. In future projects, I plan to prioritize initial training and clear guidelines to maximize productivity from the outset.

Furthermore, the Austin Area Arts website redesign successfully improved the user experience, thanks to positive feedback from stakeholders on the general layout and three company banners on the homepage. Stakeholders expressed concerns regarding the distracting nature of the heroscape and future events, as well as preferences for typeface changes. This iterative feedback demonstrates the commitment to fine-tuning the user experience based on stakeholder views, directing future projects for greater satisfaction.
